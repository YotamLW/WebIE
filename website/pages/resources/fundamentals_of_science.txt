<h1>Fundamentals of Science</h1>

<h2>Units</h2>
<p>Units give values practical context. SI (International Standard) units are standard. The 7 SI base units (listed below) are the most basic units that all others can be made from. All other SI units can be constructed using the base units. All other units can be written as SI units multiplied by a scale (conversion) factor. Powers of units are different units, for example, area (a power of length) cannot be converted into units of length.</p>
<li>Length - Metre [m]</li>
<li>Mass - Kilogram [kg]</li>
<li>Time - Second [s]</li>
<li>Electrical Current - Ampere [A]</li>
<li>Temperature - Kelvin [K]</li>
<li>Luminous Intenstity - Candela [cd]</li>
<li>Amount of Substance - Mole [mol]</li>

<p>The convention for writing complex units is to have them next to each other with exponents (e.g. $\um\us^{-2}$, $\ukg\um^2\us^{-2}$, $\ukg\um^2\us^{-3}\uA^{-1}$ etc.). Dimensional analysis is the field of maths concerned with conversion and consistency of units. Units are treated as variables so if $1\ukg=1000\u g$ then $5\ukg=5(1000\u g)=5000\u g$. Dimensional analysis can also involve solving for unkown exponents of a proposed equation. For example, an equation for time $t=kd^\alpha v^\beta$ is proposed where $k$ is a dimensionless constant, $d$ is distance and $v$ is velocity. An equation with the units can then be formed and the exponents solved by simultaneous equations. For the given example $[\us]=[\um]^\alpha[\um\us^{-1}]^\beta=[\um^{\alpha+\beta}\us^{-\beta}]$ so $\alpha=1$ and $\beta=-1$.</p>
